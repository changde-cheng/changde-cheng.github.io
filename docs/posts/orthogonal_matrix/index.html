What is rotation matrix

Author

Changde Cheng

Published

June 9, 2017

I find rotation matrices fascinating. A rotation matrix can represent a rotation in Euclidean space. A rotation matrix is an orthogonal matrix with determinant 1.

A rotation in two dimension through an angle \(\theta\) can be represented as: \[R(\theta)= \begin{pmatrix} \cos \theta & -\sin \theta \\ \sin \theta & \phantom{-}\cos \theta \end{pmatrix}\]

A reverse rotation is: \[R(-\theta)= \begin{pmatrix} \phantom{-}\cos \theta & \sin \theta \\ -\sin \theta & \cos \theta \end{pmatrix}\]

Note that \(R(-\theta) = R(\theta)^{\mathrm T} = R(\theta)^{-1}\), which tells that \(R(\theta\) is orthogonal. Another thing to note is that two dimension rotation have one-dimension representation through \(\theta\).

We find the set of rotation matrices as: \[SO(n) = \{\mathbf R \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}: \mathbf{R R^T = I}, ~\det{\mathbf R} = 1 \}.\]

With matrix product operation, the set in becomes a group: Special Orthogonal Group. Recall that a group is a set with a binary operation, satisfying several rules (associativity, identity element, inverser element). The rotation group \(SO(n)\) is a continuous group (Lie group).

Now take a look at \(SO(2)\). Consider two rotations \(R(\alpha)\) and \(R(\beta)\). We calculate the product of two rotations, and we guess it is \(R(\alpha + \beta)\). But let us do a calculation: \[R(\alpha) R(\beta) = \begin{pmatrix} \cos{\alpha} & -\sin{\alpha} \\ \sin{\alpha} & \cos{\alpha} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \cos{\beta} & -\sin{\beta} \\ \sin{\beta} & \cos{\beta} \end{pmatrix}= \begin{pmatrix} \cos{(\alpha+\beta)} & -\sin{(\alpha+\beta)} \\ \sin{(\alpha+\beta)} & \cos{(\alpha+\beta)} \end{pmatrix}\] This result gives us something extra: commutativity \(R(\alpha) R(\beta) = R(\alpha+\beta) =R(\beta) R(\alpha)\). In other words, \(SO(2)\) is abelian.

But \(SO(3)\) is not abelian. Consider two elements in \(SO(3)\):

\[R_a = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 & 0\\ 1 & 0 &0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix},\]

and

\[R_b = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}.\]

The two matrices are constructed by embedding the elements in \(SO(2)\) in a \(3\times 3\) matrix.

Now we calculate \(R_a R_b\):

\[R_a R_b = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix},\]

and \(R_b R_a\):

\[R_b R_a = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}.\]

We found that \(R_a R_b \ne R_b R_a\), and \(SO(3)\) is not abelian.
